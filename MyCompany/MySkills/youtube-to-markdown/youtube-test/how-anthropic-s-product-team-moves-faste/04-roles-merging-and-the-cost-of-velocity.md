# Roles Merging and the Cost of Velocity

> Engineers, PMs, and designers are converging into one role — and Anthropic is leaning into it. Product taste is the rare durable skill, humans still bring common sense and EQ, the cost of speed is product consistency, and the team copes with the chaos by being low-ego and well-rested.

## Key Points
- **Roles are merging**: PMs ship code, engineers PM, designers do both. Many Cloud Code engineers go from "see Twitter feedback" to "ship feature end of week" with **no PM involvement**.
- Anthropic's hiring stance: prefer **engineers with great product taste** over hiring more PMs. Designers on the team have also been front-end engineers.
- **Product taste is the durable skill** because "as code becomes much cheaper to write, the thing that becomes more valuable is deciding what to write."
- An **engineering background is useful for the next few months** because effort estimation feeds prioritization — but the value of any specific skill turns over every few months as model capabilities jump.
- **Humans still provide three things models lack**: common sense across launch logistics, stakeholder/EQ awareness, and the ability to wear multiple hats with low ego.
- **Coping with chaos**: lean into it, hire industry veterans who've seen ups and downs, sleep well, accept that today's P0 will be next week's footnote, and **brutally prioritize**.
- The **explicit cost of shipping fast is product consistency**: features overlap, naming is messier, users feel a "treadmill" of launches.
- **/powerup is the response** — a built-in onboarding flow added against the team's earlier principle of "no onboarding," because there are now too many features for users to discover unaided.
- Cat: "we ship products that aren't as polished as I wish they were. But our top goal is to help empower professional developers." Buggy launches are now livable when feedback loops are short.

## Details

**The Venn diagram is collapsing.** Cat: "All of the roles are merging. PMs are doing some engineering work, engineers are doing PM work, designers are PMing and also landing code." Anthropic is pretty focused on hiring engineers with great product taste — "this way we can reduce the amount of overhead for shipping any product. There are many engineers on our team who are fully able to end-to-end go from see user feedback on Twitter through to ship a product at the end of the week with almost no product involvement." Cat herself was an engineer for many years, briefly a VC, and almost all PMs on her team have either been engineers or ship code on Cloud Code.

**Why product taste is the durable skill.** Anthropic gets "tens of thousands of GitHub issues asking for every single thing under the sun. It takes a lot of care and taste to figure out which of these is worth building and what is the right way to build it." That filter — what to build and how — is the rare skill, regardless of background.

**Why engineering specifically helps right now.** "If you have an engineering background, you have a better sense for how hard something should be. And that's often a factor in what you choose to build. So if something is very easy to build, then maybe instead of debating it, you just spend an hour doing it. But if something is harder to build and you know that upfront... it helps with prioritization." Cat hedges with "for the next few months" — not because Mythos will change everything, but because every few months brings a coding-capability jump that re-prices skills.

**What humans still uniquely contribute.** Three things, in Cat's words: (1) common sense — "there's like a thousand moving pieces to any product launch... a lot that could potentially go wrong"; (2) stakeholder map / EQ — the model "doesn't always have a great sense of who all the stakeholders are, how they relate to each other, what their preferences are, what are the right venues to communicate with them"; (3) the meta-skill of operating amidst constant change — sensing where the work is, jumping in, no ego about which "role" the work technically belongs to.

**Calmness as a hiring signal.** "Our team is full of people who lean into the chaos. We try to face every challenge with a smile because there's always so much going on... if you get too stressed about anything you'll burn out." The "P0 → P00 → P0000" phenomenon (Sunday's emergency is Monday afternoon's footnote) requires sleeping well, brutally prioritizing, and accepting that some launches won't be polished. The reference image Lenny lands on: the gif from Pirates of the Caribbean of someone calmly walking down the stairs while the ship collapses around him.

**The price tag for speed is product consistency.** "Historically, when code was expensive to write, you would carefully plan out everything in your product suite, how every product relates to each other, what the use case for every single one is, how they integrate, and you would pretty much have one product for each use case. Now... we do sometimes have features that overlap with each other. A lot of the times it's because there's two form factors that we love internally and we want the external audience to tell us which one is better." For new users, the cost is discoverability — "what is the best path to accomplish X" — and a feeling that they need to check Twitter daily to keep up.

**/powerup walks back an earlier principle.** The team historically resisted onboarding flows on the grounds that the product should be intuitive. They eventually shipped /powerup — a guided tour of best practices and core features — because users explicitly asked for "100 features, what are the 10 I absolutely need to use?" Cat names this as a divergence from the original "no onboarding" stance, and treats it as evidence the team adjusts when the data demands it.
